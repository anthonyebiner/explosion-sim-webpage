Realistic Explosion Animation Using Navier-Stokes Equation for Inviscid Flow

CS184 Spring 2023, Final Project Proposal

In this project, we plan to develop a 3D animated explosion simulation program that models hot gasses as individual particles that move independently and form fires at high heat. Each particle will move independently and show color based on its temperature according to the wavelength they emit. Explosions will be smoothed using various methods to make the visualization more realistic, including a one-phase Navier-Stokes solver to approximate the motion and shape of the explosion. We will then simulate explosions in several interesting solid shapes.

Team Members: Anthony Ebiner, Shruteek Mairal, Srisai Nachuri, Maddy Chen

Problem Description

A very common rendering task in animation is an accurate rendering of flames and explosions in a wide variety of applications such as cinematography, video games, and physics simulations. However, such renderings are difficult due to fire inherently not having defined shape boundaries, moving rapidly, and being contingent on more variables such as temperature and interface with the surrounding environment. The methods for simulating fire are challenging in their own ways: A fluid simulation model may look more realistic, but is a lot more complicated in terms of physics and computation. On the other hand, a particle model for flames is easier to compute but a lot more challenging to render photo-realistically.

We are choosing to model flames and explosions based off of a particle simulation model. Particle simulations are useful for rendering flames because they help form an approximation for rapidly changing shape of a flame with perceptually random behavior. To further improve the appearance of the flames, we will use a one-phase Navier-Stokes solver to approximate the motion and shape of the explosion as a fluid-based model. Additionally, we will try to add ray tracing to improve the rendering.

Goals and Deliverables

What we plan to deliver

In this project, we plan to deliver a realistic rendering of arbitrary flames and explosions. We will code a renderer for drawing a scene of objects and lights to a 2D image framebuffer; a modeling system for physically simulating particles as well as visually representing them as fluids; a physics system for handling the velocities, temperatures, and forces that govern the explosions; and an interface for linking these three systems together. We will code the interface first as a team to ensure all modules properly interconnect. In the renderer, we plan to do projection-type rendering of particles, objects, and meshes to generate the 2D images. In the particle system, we plan to simulate particle positions, identities (species), temperatures, and colors, as well as blend them into fluid visuals. Finally, in the physics system, we plan to account for velocity, gravity, pressure, and temperature, as well as the generating particles going in random directions from a single point with high temperature to represent a point explosion. We will measure our system based on several metrics: the density of particles we are able to simulate; the amount of time each image takes to render; and qualitatively, how realistic the explosions look on observation. For actual deliverables, we will simulate 2D images and animations of rendered point explosions in various simple scenes, coloring fluids based on the temperature of their vapor and showing the particles moving with time. We plan to answer the question, “How realistic and computationally efficient are point-source explosions given our simplifying assumptions?”

What we hope to deliver

If time permits, we hope to expand our renderer to include ray tracing, accounting for lights (and with light being generated by the physics system in the process of the explosion). A renderer improvement we hope to try as well would be to extend the renderer with CUDA-accelerated raytracing. We also hope to deliver explosions in scenes where they are surrounded by immobile objects, which would simulate particle movement in more complex paths. In the physics system, we hope to account for the chemistry of the explosion, including particle generation/consumption and heat generation. One last stretch goal would be to extend the particle system with implicit surface generation to generate more interesting images. We may also try to add a separate GUI module to allow the user to customize the variables such as temperature, velocity, and forces as an alternative way to present our simulation.
